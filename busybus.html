BusyBus

BusyBus is an app concept to improve the current DC Metro and Bus app.

Roles

User Research

Figma

The Problem

Transit officials have identified a problem they would like to solve. Due to expansion, numerous bus routes have been recently added and many of those routes stop at the same bus stop. Riders want to know what the next arriving bus is and how much time they have to get to the bus stop. Simply rushing to the stop when you see a bus coming no longer works because it might not be the bus the rider is expecting. Riders are currently complaining the most about the bus stop at Washington and State, which has seven bus lines serving the stop.

The Solution

BusyBus is a mobile iOS application (a single screen in this case) to help the bus riders to know what the next arriving bus is and how much time they have to get to their respective bus stop. The app shows live estimates for any stop/station while showing the fastest route to get from point A to point B.

The Process

Research and Discovery

I needed to uncover how often people utilize public transportation and their most frequented methods (bus, metro, ridesharing apps), and main annoyances when accessing transit apps.

Key Takeaways:
  • Respondents were most frustrated with unreliability, overcrowding, length arrival times, and inaccurate bus schedules
  • 70% used a mobile app to plan their trips
  • Travelers used mobile apps for real-time status updates, and estimating departure/arrival times.

Competitive Analysis

As a result of an analysis of two competitors within the market (Google Maps & Moovit), I came up with interesting ideas on how a new competitor, BusyBus, could enter the market.

Google Maps was the leading app that my user participants used for planning their trips. Offering public transit data in and out of metropolitan areas, users are given the ability to download maps for offline use. However, Google Maps lacks in providing real-time public transit information and walking navigation directions can be inaccurate.

Moovit, a community-driven app, gives users access to real-time transit data but is limited for those who need driving directions/public transit information in regions where transit options are limited. coupled with the fact that the app doesn’t allow users to manually update their route while in use.

User Stories

User stories help clarify the scope of the app and what the most important functions of the app are. These were from three different perspectives: a new user, a returning user, and all users, and ranked in importance of high, medium, or low.

User Testing

The created three-screen paper prototype of the proposed BusyBus application was tested with three potential users. Based on a usability test script , the paper prototype has been tested and modified based on the findings and feedbacks from the users including lack of user location within the app, icon sizing, and concern for lack of tool & navigation bars.

Conclusion

Using HTML, CSS, GitHub, and Atom, the main screen of Busy Bus has been developed. I needed to make sure that based on my user survey results, I was including pertinent information, not just information to fulfill the project brief. I learned to put myself in the shoes of a commuter using this app, and determined all the information needed to complete a trip (ie., disance to the stop nearest to me & and a subsequent walking estimate. T Such small details, set a product apart from the competition within the satured market.

I utilized Google’s Material Design principles to assist in creating the BusyBus within the guidelines, assisting me in the creation of an effecient and thorough good user experience within a time sensitive case.
